Normalizing Flows

Introduction to Generative Models​

Main types of generative models:

  • Autoregressive models
  • Variational Autoencoders (VAE)
  • Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)

Issues:

  • Autoregressive models are slow because they generate elements sequentially.
  • VAEs optimize ELBO instead of true likelihood.
  • GANs are powerful but unstable and less effective for tasks like speech generation.

Reference: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uXY18nzdSsM

Flow-based Models​

Flow-based models optimize the log-likelihood of data pG(x)p_G(x)pG​(x) transformed from a Gaussian distribution π(z)\pi(z)π(z):

z→G→xz \rightarrow G \rightarrow xz→G→x

Objective:

G∗=arg⁡max⁡G∑i=1mlog⁡pG(xi)G^* = \arg\max_{G} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \log p_G(x^i)G∗=argmaxG​∑i=1m​logpG​(xi)

Equivalent to minimizing:

arg⁡min⁡G∑imDKL(pdata∥pG)\arg\min_G \sum_{i}^{m} D_{KL}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_G)argminG​∑im​DKL​(pdata​∥pG​)

Flow-based models directly optimize log-likelihood.

Jacobian Matrix​

Given:

x=f(z),z=[z1z2],x=[x1x2]\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z}), \quad \mathbf{z} = \begin{bmatrix} z_1 \\ z_2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}x=f(z),z=[z1​z2​​],x=[x1​x2​​]

Jacobian:

Jf=[∂x1∂z1∂x1∂z2∂x2∂z1∂x2∂z2]J_f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_1} & \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial z_2} \\ \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial z_1} & \frac{\partial x_2}{\partial z_2} \end{bmatrix}Jf​=[∂z1​∂x1​​∂z1​∂x2​​​∂z2​∂x1​​∂z2​∂x2​​​]

Inverse Jacobian:

Jf−1=[∂z1∂x1∂z1∂x2∂z2∂x1∂z2∂x2]J_{f^{-1}} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial x_2} \\ \frac{\partial z_2}{\partial x_1} & \frac{\partial z_2}{\partial x_2} \end{bmatrix}Jf−1​=[∂x1​∂z1​​∂x1​∂z2​​​∂x2​∂z1​​∂x2​∂z2​​​]

Relationship:

Jf−1⋅Jf=IJ_{f^{-1}} \cdot J_f = IJf−1​⋅Jf​=I

Determinant of Jacobian​

For an invertible matrix AAA:

det⁡(A−1)=1det⁡(A)\det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\det(A)}det(A−1)=det(A)1​

Thus:

det⁡(Jf−1)=1det⁡(Jf)\det(J_{f^{-1}}) = \frac{1}{\det(J_f)}det(Jf−1​)=det(Jf​)1​

Why Coupling Layers​

Problems:

  1. High-dimensional Jacobians make determinants expensive.
  2. Training requires G−1G^{-1}G−1, so GGG must be invertible and dimension-preserving.
  3. Need multiple invertible transformations → flows.

Coupling Layers​

We apply a sequence of invertible transformations:

zi=G1−1(G2−1(⋯GK−1(xi)))z^i = G_1^{-1}(G_2^{-1}(\cdots G_K^{-1}(x^i)))zi=G1−1​(G2−1​(⋯GK−1​(xi)))

Density:

pK(xi)=π(zi)∏h=1K∣det⁡(JGh−1)∣p_K(x^i) = \pi(z^i) \prod_{h=1}^{K} |\det(J_{G_h^{-1}})|pK​(xi)=π(zi)h=1∏K​∣det(JGh−1​​)∣

Log density:

log⁡pK(xi)=log⁡π(zi)+∑h=1Klog⁡∣det⁡(JGh−1)∣\log p_K(x^i) = \log \pi(z^i) + \sum_{h=1}^{K} \log|\det(J_{G_h^{-1}})|logpK​(xi)=logπ(zi)+h=1∑K​log∣det(JGh−1​​)∣

During training we optimize G−1G^{-1}G−1 but use GGG for generation.
